Evidence-based simulation — and a new instrument for pain.

Decades of research show that simulation-based training builds clinical skill and carries through to patient care.1 DevPT brings that to physical therapy across a set of practice tools — and pushes past the classroom with PainMap, a research instrument for capturing pain in three dimensions.

The case for simulation

Across more than 600 studies, technology-enhanced simulation produced large gains in knowledge and skills — and moderate gains in patient outcomes.1

JAMA, 2011
Proven in physical therapy

Replacing up to a quarter of clinical placement with simulation produced comparable student competency across the core domains of PT practice.2

Physical Therapy, 2016
Sharper reasoning

Virtual patients strengthen clinical reasoning — especially history-taking, data gathering, and management decisions.3

// research instrument · in development

PainMap: pain in three dimensions.

Pain is still mostly recorded as a single number from 0 to 10 — a measure that only loosely tracks the experience and misses its quality, its spread, and how it changes over time.4 Even digital pain maps are usually flat, two-dimensional body charts.5

  • Whole-body, in 3D — pain placed on a real model, not a flat outline.
  • Quality, not just intensity — sharp, dull, burning, tingling, and how far it spreads.
  • Change over time — pain that migrates and evolves across days and weeks.
  • Research-grade — built for clinical pain science, not only the classroom.

It began as a teaching idea and has become a serious instrument for clinical pain science — aiming well past where digital pain measurement stands today. It's already live and in use at pain3d.com.

Tools for learning the clinic.

Each gives students reps at part of the encounter — documentation, the patient interview, imaging, and hands-on therapy — in a place where mistakes cost nothing.

emrsim · live

EMRsim

Documentation that behaves like a real chart.

Students read charts and write notes in an environment modeled on a real EMR — so clinical documentation is familiar before the first rotation, not learned on the fly in front of a patient.

Open EMRsim →

vspx · live

VSPx Chat

A virtual patient to interview, on demand.

Students take a history and reason through a case that responds to their questions — unlimited repetitions without a standardized patient's schedule, exactly where virtual patients show the clearest benefit.3

Open VSPx Chat →

simpacs · live

simPACS

The imaging workstation, hands-on.

X-ray, CT, MRI, and ultrasound to scroll through, window, and interpret — so that reading imaging becomes something students have practiced, not just watched on a lecture slide.

Open simPACS →

aquatic · live

Aquatic Therapy

How the body responds to water.

Depth, buoyancy, hydrostatic pressure, and temperature modeled together, live — building intuition for how immersion changes the body before anyone is at the poolside.

Open Aquatic Therapy →

lab · live

DevPT Lab

The whole encounter, start to finish.

Where the other tools come together: take a history from a virtual patient, work the chart, document the visit, and commit to a plan — competency-based clinical missions run as one continuous case, as many times as it takes.
